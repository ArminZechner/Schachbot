So what can it actually do? Three game modes: classic one-versus-one with two keypads; player against the AI online, where we send the board to the Stockfish engine and get the best move back; and an offline mode so it still works without WiFi. It knows the complete chess rules, including the tricky ones like castling and en passant, and it catches illegal moves. The flow of a single move is simple for the player: you type the start and target square, the logic checks it, and if it's legal the gantry first slides under the piece, then the electromagnet grabs it and drags it across. That's the whole magic. Arthur will now show you the hardware that makes it possible.
Part 1 · Arthur P     

The Hardware

One ESP32, a hand-milled circuit board, and the electronics that turn a move into motion.

Thanks Daniel. I'm Arthur and I took care of the electronics, the heart of the whole system. I'll walk you through the brain, the circuit board I designed and milled myself, and the building blocks that drive the motors and the magnet.

Simple Electronics

  • 2 × 4×4 keypads for entering moves
  • A MOSFET switching the electromagnet
  • Stop buttons for homing
Everything is controlled by a single ESP32. I chose it because it's powerful, cheap, and it has built-in WiFi which we need for the online AI. Around it sit the parts the players interact with: two four-by-four keypads where they type their moves, a MOSFET that switches the electromagnet on and off, and a couple of stop buttons that the gantry uses to find its zero position. I'll come to the motors, drivers and display in a moment. My job was to connect all of this reliably on one board.

A self-designed, self-milled PCB

From schematic → single-sided layout → milled in-house. We even hid a little chess king in the copper. ♚

This is the part I'm most proud of. I drew the complete schematic in EasyEDA, then turned it into a single-sided layout, meaning all the copper tracks are on one side, which is much harder to route because tracks can't cross. We milled the board ourselves here at school. One of our professors actually called the layout "beautiful", and yes, we hid a tiny chess king in the copper as a signature. Designing this board taught me a lot: we even had to redesign it a couple of times because we'd forgotten components.

Motor drivers

Two stepper drivers turn the ESP32's pulses into precise step-and-direction signals for the X and Y axis.

12 V stepper motors

Two 12 V steppers spin the toothed belts that move the gantry across the board.

Display

A 3.5" SPI screen (480×320) shows whose turn it is and how the board is "seen".

Let me quickly walk through the power and motion side. Power comes from a 24-volt supply for the electromagnet, and step-down converters create the lower voltages, 12 volts for the motors and 5 volts for the ESP32 and display. The motor drivers translate the controller's pulses into precise step-and-direction signals. Those drive two 12-volt stepper motors that pull the toothed belts and move the gantry. And the display is a three-and-a-half inch SPI screen that mirrors the board. Every one of these I tested individually before putting them on the final board.

The magnet trick

Every piece has a small permanent magnet glued into its base. Under the board, an electromagnet on the carriage switches on and the piece sticks to the square above it, then it gets dragged along.

The tuning challenge

Strong enough to pull one piece, gentle enough not to drag its neighbours.

Solved with ~5 × ~5 cm squares and a small, precisely sized coil.

The clever bit is how pieces actually move. Each piece has a little permanent magnet in its 3D-printed base. Under the board, the gantry carries an electromagnet. When we switch the coil on, the piece above it sticks to the board and we can drag it across to any square. The real challenge was tuning the strength: strong enough to pull the target piece, but weak enough that it doesn't drag the neighbouring pieces along. We solved it with five-by-five centimetre squares and a small, precisely sized coil. And that's the hardware, Armin will now explain the software that decides every move.
Part 2 · Armin Z    

The Program

1,837 lines of C++ on the ESP32, rules, motion, display and the chess engine, all working together.

Thanks Arthur. I'm Armin, the team leader, and I worked mostly on the software. Everything you see the machine "decide" lives in 1,837 lines of C++ running on the ESP32. I'll show you the structure and the four interesting jobs the code does, kept readable.

Built as clean, separate modules

  • Chess, the rule book & board state
  • Gantry, turns moves into motor steps
  • Display, draws the live board
  • Keypad, reads the two matrices
  • StockfishAPI, talks to the engine
  • WiFi, manages the connection

Tied together by one non-blocking main loop, so the motors keep moving while everything else runs.

I split the program into clean modules, each with one job: Chess holds the rules and the board, Gantry turns a move into motor steps, Display draws the board, Keypad reads the matrices, StockfishAPI talks to the engine, and WiFi manages the connection. The trick is the main loop on the right, it never blocks. Every pass it nudges the motors, keeps WiFi alive, reads a key, and only when a full move is entered does it validate it, apply it, redraw, and in AI mode play the computer's answer. Non-blocking means the motors stay smooth while everything else happens.

A complete chess rule-book

For every move the engine asks four questions before anything moves:

  • Is there a piece on the start square?
  • Is it your colour & your turn?
  • Does it obey that piece's movement rule?
  • Would it leave your own king in check?

Special cases it gets right

  • Castling & en passant
  • Pawn promotion
  • Check & checkmate detection
  • Illegal input, clear error & retry

Every move is verified before a single motor turns.

The biggest piece of software is the rule engine. For every move it checks: is there a piece on the start square, is it your colour, does the move obey that specific piece's movement rule, and crucially would it leave your own king in check. On top of that it handles the special cases, castling, en passant and promotion. If anything is wrong, it returns a clear reason and the display tells the player to try again, or announces check and checkmate. Writing this so it catches every illegal move took the most testing of the whole project.

Asking a grandmaster engine

To get a strong move, we translate our board into a FEN string and send it to the public Stockfish API at depth 15, it replies with the best move, like e7e5.

No internet? We fall back to a random legal move, so the game never stops.

what a FEN looks like

rnbqkbnr/pppppppp/8/8/
4P3/8/PPPP1PPP/
RNBQKBNR w

Each rank, piece-by-piece, plus whose turn it is, the universal language every chess engine speaks.

For a genuinely strong opponent we don't compute moves ourselves, we ask Stockfish, one of the best chess engines in the world. The key is translation: we convert our internal board into a FEN string, which is the standard text format every engine understands. We send it over HTTPS to the public Stockfish API with a search depth of fifteen, and it replies with the best move, like "e7e5". If there's no internet, we simply fall back to a random legal move, so the game keeps working. Converting the board to FEN correctly, counting empty squares, lower-case for black, was a fiddly but satisfying bit of code.

Turning a move into motion

The code keeps two pictures of the board, the target (how it should look) and the real one, then drags pieces until they match.

How it closes the gap

  • Scan all 64 squares, find every difference
  • Captures first, taken piece → graveyard
  • Then drag each piece to its new square
  • Repeat until real == target
How a move becomes motion. The code keeps two boards: the target, how it should look, and the real one. It scans all sixty-four squares, finds what's different, and drags pieces until reality matches the target. Captures are handled first: the taken piece is dragged off to a graveyard on the side before the capturing piece moves in. On the next slide I'll show the clever part, how it avoids knocking pieces over.

Weaving between the pieces

Instead of pushing a piece straight through the centre of other squares, the magnet steps half a square onto the grid lines and travels along them, so it never knocks a neighbour over.

Calibrating those steps so the piece always lands dead-centre took real effort.

And the clever detail: instead of pushing a piece straight through the centre of other squares, the magnet steps half a square onto the grid lines and weaves between the pieces, so it never knocks anything over. Calibrating those steps so the piece always lands dead-centre took real effort. That's the software, back to Daniel for the 3D design.
Part 3 · Daniel S    

3D Design & Build

The mechanical heart: a self-built 2D gantry and dozens of custom 3D-printed parts.

Thanks Armin. So all that software needs a body to move, and that was my main technical job: designing and building the 2D gantry, the casing and the printed parts. Let me show you the mechanics.

A 2D gantry, designed from scratch

  • X & Y motor + idler mounts
  • A stiff transition plate linking both axes
  • A carriage carrying the electromagnet
The gantry is the mechanical heart. It's the same principle as a pen-plotter or a 3D-printer: two stepper motors with toothed belts move a carriage in X and Y, so the magnet can reach any of the sixty-four squares from underneath. I designed it completely from scratch in CAD. Every single motor, every pulley and the carriage itself sits in a custom 3D-printed bracket that I modelled, the motor mounts, the idler mounts, a stiff transition plate that links the two axes, and the carriage that carries the magnet. The initial gantry was working in under five weeks, which we were really proud of.

Casing, frame & the playing field

Around the gantry I designed all the casing. A two-part case holds the display at a comfortable viewing angle for both players, each keypad gets its own angled holder, and ten hollow pillars raise the plexiglass playing field above the gantry, that gap is exactly where the magnet travels. The casing and the clear playing field came out really clean once assembled; that's one of the things I'm happiest with.

Precision & a lot of printing

Every square had to line up perfectly, so the design started with the geometry of the board, square size, frame, and the exact steps-per-field the motors need.

  • Designed in Onshape & Fusion 360
  • Sliced in Bambu Studio, printed on a Bambu Lab A1
  • Big parts took 7+ hours each to print
Precision was everything: if the squares don't line up, the magnet grabs the wrong piece. So I started with the pure geometry, the square size, the frame, and the exact number of motor steps per field. I designed in Onshape and Fusion 360, sliced in Bambu Studio and printed on a Bambu Lab A1. Some of the big parts took over seven hours each to print, so planning what to print and when really mattered. Getting the tolerances right was the hardest mechanical lesson of the project. What we take away

What went well

  • Working 2D gantry in under 5 weeks
  • A “beautiful” single-sided milled PCB
  • Clean casing & plexiglass field
  • Robust, modular software architecture

What we learned

  • Decide on the materials & technology at the start
  • Mechanical tolerances need calibration
  • Tuning the magnet was harder than expected
  • Clear roles & communication matter
To wrap up, what we're proud of: we had a working gantry in under five weeks, a milled circuit board a professor literally called beautiful, a clean case, and a solid software architecture. And what we learned: decide on the materials and technology right at the start, respect mechanical tolerances, the magnet tuning was trickier than we thought, and clear roles in a team really matter.
